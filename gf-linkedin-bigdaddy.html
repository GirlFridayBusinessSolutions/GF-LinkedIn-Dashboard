LinkedIn Algorithm: How Reach Actually Works

Why some posts reach 600 people and others reach 15
What Kills Reach
🔗
Links early in the caption. LinkedIn penalizes outbound links by 40–60% reach. The scheduler-compatible solve: put the link at the very bottom of the caption, after hashtags, on its own line. The algorithm scans early content for links — burying it last reduces the penalty significantly without changing the workflow.
✏️
Editing after posting. The algorithm interprets an edit as a signal the post wasn’t ready and resets distribution to near zero. Proof before scheduling. No exceptions.
⏱️
Slow first-hour engagement. LinkedIn distributes to a small test group first. If that group engages quickly, distribution expands. If it’s slow, the post stalls. The first 60 minutes are the most critical window of any post’s life.
🔁
Repeating the same CTA language. The algorithm and the audience both tune out repeated patterns. Rotate CTA wording every post — variety signals fresh content.
📅
Inconsistent posting. A pause resets some distribution momentum. Always restart after a break with the strongest possible hook — not an easy post.
What Accelerates Reach
💬
Comment velocity in the first hour. Comments are the highest-weight signal the algorithm receives. A post that generates 3 comments in the first hour will outreach a post with 20 likes and no comments. Takisha, Kimmie, and Amy engaging in the first 15 minutes has a real and measurable effect on distribution.
🔖
Saves. When someone saves a post, LinkedIn flags it as high-value content. Saves are invisible in analytics but are a powerful reach multiplier. Framework posts and checklists earn saves. Thought leadership earns likes. Both matter for different reasons.
🔄
Shares from outside your network. When someone who doesn’t follow GirlFriday shares a post, LinkedIn expands distribution beyond the existing follower base. This is how the Feb 20 post reached 632 people. Free value carousels earn shares. Thought leadership earns reposts.
⌛
Dwell time. How long someone reads a post factors into distribution even without engaging. Multi-slide carousels and long-form posts increase dwell time significantly over short text posts.
🏢
Internal team engagement early. When Takisha, Kimmie, and Amy like or comment on a post within the first hour, it signals credibility to the algorithm. Three people engaging in the first 15 minutes can meaningfully expand the distribution window.

What Didn’t — Dec 29 to Mar 25, 2026

Gratitude-led testimonials. "We’re honored," "this means so much" earned C or D without exception. Lead with the client outcome.

Posts with no link or CTA. Good content without a next step generates goodwill and no clicks. Every post needs a direction.
The Winning Post Formula (Updated April 2026)
1
Open with a scene or a declarative statement. Put the reader inside a specific moment they recognize, or say something true that makes them stop. Not a formula. Example: "The pipeline looks healthy. The calls are happening. Revenue is still unpredictable month to month." They know this feeling. You’ve just described their Tuesday.
2
Name the specific mechanism. Not "the problem is structure." Tell them exactly where it breaks. "The break is almost never at the top of the funnel — it’s in the middle, where the process takes over and the process was never documented."
3
Bridge to GirlFriday without selling. One or two sentences. Not a pitch — a position. "GirlFriday comes in at exactly that point — not to evaluate the team, but to build the structure the team has been trying to operate without."
4
Proof or stakes. A specific result or consequence that makes inaction cost something. Not hypothetical. Stakes beat inspiration every time.

The 2026 shift: "I know something" content is everywhere and AI replicates it instantly. "I did something" content cannot be faked — it comes from GirlFriday’s actual client work. When a post says "a client came to us with this problem, here is what we found, here is what changed in 30 days" it becomes uncopiable. That is what separates GirlFriday from every other consultancy posting on LinkedIn.
5
Rotating CTA + buried link. Vary CTA language every post. Then: hashtags. Then a blank line. Then the link on its own line at the very bottom. The algorithm sees the link last. The audience sees it when they’re already convinced.

A/B Test Rotation

4 tests in April. Results reviewed at May dashboard build. Trigger word test previewed for May.
Week 1 (Apr 7–11) — Hook Format
Hypothesis: A scene-setting opening outperforms a declarative contrast hook because it creates immediate identification rather than intellectual agreement. Self-recognition converts faster than being told something is true.
Version A — Scene Opening
Drop the reader into a specific, recognizable situation. "Your team is working. The same problems keep landing on the same people." They identify before they engage.
Version B — Declarative Contrast
Two short sentences. A truth and its more powerful version. "Revenue growth is exciting. Sustainable revenue is strategic." The contrast is the hook.
Watch: Engagement rate + link clicks. Scene hooks tend to generate more comments. Declarative hooks tend to generate more clicks. Both matter — for different reasons.
Week 2 (Apr 14–18) — Post Length
Hypothesis: Long-form posts earn higher engagement rate because the ICA feels they received something of value. Short posts may earn more comments because there is space to respond. Apr 15 (long) vs Apr 22 (short) is the direct comparison.
Version A — Long (250+ words)
Full opening, pain, bridge, proof, CTA. Earn dwell time. Apr 15 is this version.
Version B — Short (under 80 words)
Opening observation. One specific line. Link. Nothing else. Apr 22 is this version.
Watch: Engagement rate + comments on Apr 15 vs Apr 22. Same week, same topic range, opposite lengths. Clean comparison.
Week 3 (Apr 21–25) — CTA Language
Hypothesis: A CTA that names the specific resource outcome outperforms a general site CTA because the ICA knows exactly what they’re clicking toward. Specificity reduces friction.
Version A — Resource-Specific
"Full funnel breakdown at girlfridaybusinesssolutions.com" — names what they’re getting.
Version B — Site Direction
"See how GirlFriday approaches this: girlfridaybusinesssolutions.com" — names what they’re doing.
Watch: Clicks vs impressions ratio across the week. Informs which CTA framing to use for every lead magnet post in May.
Week 4 (Apr 28–30) — Content Angle
Hypothesis: A post that gives away a specific framework earns more reach than one that argues a position, because saves and shares expand distribution beyond the follower base. Apr 18 carousel (free value) vs Apr 29 text post (thought leadership) is the comparison.
Version A — Free Value (Apr 18)
Give away something useful in the post itself. Watch: saves, shares, new follows.
Version B — Thought Leadership (Apr 29)
Take a position. Make the argument. Watch: reposts, comments, profile visits.
Watch: Reposts + saves across both posts. This informs how May content is split between the two angles.

On retiring trigger words for now

🤔
Trigger words work. They work well. But they work best when two conditions are true: the page has enough reach that strangers (not just followers) are seeing the post, and the brand has the infrastructure to respond consistently within hours. At 789 followers with primarily internal comment engagement in the current data, running trigger word CTAs in April would be asking Amy to manage a manual response system for a mechanic that hasn’t proven its volume yet on this page. That’s effort without evidence.
🧪
The smarter move is to establish April’s direct link performance as a clean baseline, then introduce trigger words on 2 posts in May and compare the downstream action directly. If trigger words on a page this size generate meaningfully more DMs, comments, and link visits than a buried direct link, they become a rotation. If they don’t, the data settles the question permanently and we don’t build infrastructure around something that isn’t moving the needle.
May test plan: 2 posts get trigger word CTAs. Everything else stays direct link. Compare: comments generated, DMs received, link clicks per post. Same topic range, same posting day, only the CTA mechanic changes.

On retiring the "Most X isn’t X" hook format

✍️
This was the right call and I’m glad it was flagged. That formula dominated LinkedIn content strategy for about 18 months and it still converts — but it now reads as AI-generated to a savvy executive audience. When the hook pattern is recognizable as a template, it removes the voice. And GirlFriday’s voice is what has been generating 9x benchmark engagement. The formula was never the asset. The specificity and truth behind it was.
💡
The April posts are rewritten as declarative statements and scene-setting observations. "Projects fall behind in the gaps. Not when the work is happening — when it’s supposed to move from one person to the next." Same reframe. No formula fingerprint. The ICA recognizes the scene before they engage with the argument. That self-recognition is actually a stronger conversion mechanism than intellectual agreement — and it’s harder to replicate at scale.
Watch in April: Whether the declarative/scene hooks maintain the same engagement rates as the formula hooks did. If they perform equally or better, the formula is permanently retired. If there’s a meaningful drop, we may selectively bring back the reframe structure on specific post types where it still earns its place.

On the link-in-caption question

🔗
The algorithm link penalty is real but it is not binary. LinkedIn penalizes links that appear early in the caption — specifically in the first two or three lines that appear before "see more." Burying the link after hashtags at the very bottom of the post means the algorithm reads through the entire caption before it encounters the link. The penalty exists but it’s significantly reduced compared to a link in line two. This is the practical scheduler-compatible solve and it’s how most high-performing brand pages handle it.
📱
The format for every post in the scheduler: body copy, CTA sentence, blank line, hashtags, blank line, link on its own line. That structure is in the calendar for every post. Do not put the link anywhere else. Do not put it in the first comment unless the scheduler can do it automatically — manually adding it after posting is not a viable workflow and has no meaningful reach advantage over the buried-in-caption approach for a page at this follower size.

1
Cross-Posting: GirlFriday Page + Takisha Personal Reshare with Tag
Current practice (1x weekly): post on the GirlFriday company page, then Takisha reshares to her personal profile and tags GirlFriday. This is worth keeping. The earlier experiment of daily comment seeding (posting comments from Takisha’s account to manufacture engagement velocity) was retired because the algorithm at this follower size doesn’t respond to internal cycling. The weekly reshare is different — it’s a legitimate distribution mechanism. Keep it on the highest-performing post of each week, not a rotating default.

The cleaner April approach is a direct link buried after the hashtags: no friction, no performance mechanic, just a clear path for someone who is actually interested. If May’s data shows the audience is large enough to make trigger words meaningful, we revisit. Until then, they stay out.
